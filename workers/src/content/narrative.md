# Career narrative

## Pre-JMA: research foundations and first professional exposure (2014–2018)

The professional trajectory begins at Università di Bologna, where a three-year research-track position as Cloud Solutions Designer ran concurrently with the Bachelor's and Master's programs in Electronics, Computer Science and Telecommunications Engineering. The research focus — cloud computing, SDN controller integration, and OpenStack networking performance — produced four peer-reviewed IEEE conference publications and culminated in a Best Paper Award at ICC 2018 for work on native SDN integration within OpenStack. The research was collaborative and hands-on: designing and deploying cloud clusters for EU-funded cooperative research projects, developing a performance evaluation framework for SDN controllers (Ryu, ONOS, OpenDaylight), and supervising graduating students.

A visiting scholar period at UCLA in 2015 broadened the international research exposure, and in 2017 selection for the Huawei Seeds for the Future program brought a month of telecommunications R&D immersion in Shenzhen, concurrent with a language and culture program at Beijing Language and Culture University. These experiences established an early pattern of working across distributed, multi-cultural, multi-site engineering environments that would later define the JMA years.

The transition from academic research to industry came through Laboratori Guglielmo Marconi, where eight months as a System and Network Engineer covered design, deployment, and monitoring of IP networks for public-sector and large-scale private organizations. Key deliverables included a zero-downtime migration of a city-wide hospital network and full integration of a large-scale retail operation into a 24/7 monitoring mesh. The position bridged academic research and industrial network engineering, reinforcing an operational discipline that would later inform telecom-grade system architecture work.

## JMA early years: cloud-native foundations (2018–2021)

Joining JMA Wireless in July 2018 as a Networking System Design Engineer, the mandate was deliberately exploratory: research mode, no product-delivery pressure, with the freedom to test how much the XRAN baseband processing software could realistically perform in modern execution environments from a networking standpoint. Between August and November 2018, three foundational proofs of concept took shape: an InfiniBand-based evaluation, the first PoCs of XRAN running inside Docker containers, and the first Kubernetes-based experimentation environment for XRAN components. A contribution to provisional patents (then filed) on container orchestration for BBU formalized some of the early thinking.

The conclusions of these PoCs — what was feasible, what would degrade under virtualization, where data-plane choices would constrain future architecture — became the technical foundation for the multi-year cloud-native XRAN program. A promotion to System Architect in April 2019 reflected the shift from exploratory PoCs to systematic architectural evaluation. The work deepened into BBU execution across bare metal, VMs, and containers, given the bloom of customers with different Open RAN requirements. This included investigation of low-latency data-plane options and their long-term implications. The architectural separation between BBU real-time critical paths and ancillary services was formalized, and Prometheus-based monitoring and benchmarking practices were established to support data-driven decisions rather than opinion-driven narratives.

Cisco engagements in 2019 — lab integration tests in Milan, CBRS project support, and Paris lab setup for O&M/NSO integration — brought the first sustained exposure to multi-vendor, multi-operator system integration at scale. The Cisco NCS 540 became the primary timing switch family for XRAN and O-RAN deployments from 2020 onward, and PTP configuration expertise for G.8275.1 and G.8275.2 profiles became a recurring architectural concern.

From 2020, COVID-era constraints drove a shift toward reproducible, automation-friendly setups and stronger reliance on software-based observability. The cost of fragile lab setups and undocumented assumptions became obvious and recovered just on time. Cross-site alignment with US-based labs and teams became a regular activity, with periods of on-site presence to validate integration assumptions and close gaps between architecture and implementation. By 2021, the cloud-native XRAN execution model had moved from experimental feasibility to a technically defensible, trusted foundation — surviving real stress from travel limits, partial lab access, and distributed teams.

Parallel technology and vendor threads matured during this period. NVIDIA/Mellanox NIC strategy evolved from early InfiniBand evaluation through ConnectX-6 Dx PTP testing to ConnectX-5 consolidation. Evaluation of high-performance IPSec led to hands-on benchmarking that achieved 9.1 Gbps on a 10 Gbps link. Wind River WRCP was evaluated and deployed for a major APAC operator, including kernel profile analysis around BPF vs Preempt-RT constraints. A Juniper Security Gateway integration for a European operator's Open RAN deployment was completed successfully after several debug sessions that highlighted a non-standard configuration in the IKEv2 exchange. A containerization platforms analysis comparing VMware Tanzu, Wind River WRCP, Red Hat OpenShift/OKD, and internal Kubernetes documented the CaaS/PaaS landscape as it stood.

Two threads that began quietly during this period would later define much of the mid-period and recent work. The first was third-party O-RU integration: practical M-Plane integration work with radios from multiple vendors in 2020, followed by further O-RAN radio integration in operator-facing contexts and an early evaluation of a Massive MIMO O-RU platform in 2021, progressively revealed that successful O-RU integration required operational procedures, bootstrapping details, and M-Plane configuration workflows — not only nominal standards compliance. The second was Kubernetes platform landscape evaluation: an AWS EKS + Multus PoC demonstrated that XRAN software could be reused across Kubernetes-based platforms, while SUSE/Rancher engagement sharpened the understanding of what telco-edge Kubernetes actually required in terms of timing, lifecycle, air-gap support, and operational behavior. These early platform evaluations would eventually converge toward the Red Hat OCP direction.

## JMA mid-period: system ownership and field readiness (2021–2024)

Promotion to Manager, 5G O&M and Cloud in September 2021 brought direct leadership of a ten-engineer team and expanded scope beyond XRAN execution into O-RAN management, timing architecture, vendor interoperability, and field-readiness concerns as first-class architectural items. The role formalized what had been emerging for several years: XRAN cloud-native architecture was no longer just about making the software run in containers, but about making it operable, manageable, and deployable at scale.

A major deliverable was the definition of concrete NETCONF/YANG requirements for switch management, covering configuration, L2/L3 interfaces, VLANs, timing, QoS, mirroring, fault management, and performance measurements. A deliberate push toward OpenConfig YANG model adoption ensured consistency across Fibrolan, Cisco, and Ciena. Timing architecture — PTP, SyncE, GNSS — was elevated from optional enhancement to gating prerequisite for field deployment. Vendor interoperability was managed directly: coordinating QA to validate switches under production-level traffic, structuring interop discussions to align product portfolios before engineering engagement, and exposing the gap between nominal O-RAN compliance and what was required for field operation through practical integration of MTI, Fujitsu, Mavenir, and JMA O-RAN radios. Each vendor introduced different assumptions around mapping, compression, M-Plane behavior, bootstrapping, and defaults — reinforcing the lesson that "multi-vendor O-RAN" required productized integration models, not just standards documents. The O-RU Controller matured from a management add-on into a critical system integration point requiring lifecycle handling, DHCP/call-home behavior, fault visibility, and repeatable configuration workflows. This experience directly informed later DAS/Jazz architecture thinking around O-RAN POI, Cat-A/Cat-B behavior, and Distributed Massive MIMO feasibility.

The CaaS platform landscape was assessed in parallel. AWS EKS-A and Snowball Edge were evaluated for edge-platform suitability, with significant concerns documented around Ubuntu-based VM assumptions, SR-IOV behavior, and air-gapped operations. Intel FlexRAN and ACC100 accelerator evaluations clarified the distinction between backhaul/midhaul and fronthaul NIC requirements. SUSE/Rancher engagement continued around telco-edge Kubernetes and Nephio tracking, sharpening the understanding of what a viable telco CaaS platform would need: air-gap support, lifecycle management, PTP integration, and avoidance of platform lock-in.

The transition to Principal Software Architect in February 2023 formalized the shift toward system-of-systems ownership. XRAN performance dimensioning and KPI characterization became part of the release process through System Dimensioning Guidelines as a formal milestone deliverable. The Plugfest program was structured as an integration feedback mechanism tied to architecture workshops and roadmap decisions, not as a demo exercise. On-site lab work in Boulder validated integration assumptions that remained invisible in remote or document-driven reviews.

CaaS platform assessment matured from individual vendor evaluations into a comparative architecture discussion across AWS EKS-A, Rancher/SUSE, SpectroCloud Palette, and Red Hat OpenShift, with the central question shifting from "which Kubernetes distribution works in the lab" to "which platform model can be productized, supported, automated, and operated." Red Hat OCP was proposed as the strategic PaaS direction, with a detailed PoC work plan covering manufacturing, air-gap installation, BBU workloads, networking, security, monitoring, and documentation.

The period also saw the first public-facing technical contributions at scale: the ENS Workshop keynote at IEEE NetSoft 2024 on telco edge challenges, expanded university collaboration with Università di Bologna and Saint Louis University, and initial O-RAN standards delegate participation at the Athens and Incheon F2F meetings in 2024. Four patents were granted or published during this period, covering virtual base station orchestration, multi-operator fronthaul, shared spectrum access, and blockchain-based network security.

## JMA recent: architecture leadership and convergence (2024–present)

The transition to Director, Software Architecture in August 2024 broadened scope to encompass technology strategy, research, standards engagement, and research initiatives across carrier, enterprise, and federal / defense market segments. Product research around Jazz DAS became the dominant architecture thread, while XRAN governance, platform assessment, and standards engagement continued in parallel.

Jazz DAS architecture planning covered RF-to-eCPRI POI variants, O-RAN POI variants, RU/switch/supervisor integration, and PoC framing. Advanced development inputs addressed BBU platform evolution. Integration validation needs were identified, reconnecting earlier multi-vendor O-RU and timing lessons into the DAS/Jazz architecture.

Red Hat/OCP engagement deepened into a technical-commercial. RHEL for Edge, bootc, and MicroShift were evaluated for DAS/Jazz deployments requiring air-gapped upgrades and smaller footprint.

Standards engagement moved from intermittent spare-time tracking to a structured strategic proposal. A standards strategy package recommended a sustainable 3GPP/O-RAN monitoring and participation model with FTE justification, framing standards participation as a source of specification insight, partner credibility, and IP opportunity rather than a reactive adaptation to specifications already decided by others. Delegate attendance continued at O-RAN F2F meetings in Dallas (2025) and Rome (2026), and at the 3GPP RAN Plenary 111 in Fukuoka (2026).

An AI Copilot pilot was structured for DAS 3.0 R&D with concrete governance: KPI tracking, monthly review checkpoints, and management-facing material to defend the initiative in budget discussions. 

University and research collaboration remained active throughout: guest lectures at Syracuse University and Università di Bologna across multiple courses, a network automation lab with hands-on NETCONF and Ansible material, and seminars on distributed systems at the edge. Internal knowledge transfer continued through architecture workshops, standards briefings, and training series on topics from XRAN cloud-native architecture to AI-RAN Alliance roadmaps.
